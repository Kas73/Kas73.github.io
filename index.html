Problem

The elderly have limited social interaction because they don't go outside as much and this causes them to feel sad and lonely. Furthermore remaining healthy is a significant challenge once one is older with many not being able to track their medication and barely doing exercise contributing to their deteriorating health. Once the COVID-19 pandemic happened these problems were compounded to a greater extent making the situation for elderly people much more dire. Designing applications that the elderly can easily use is a challenge as well because they are not as adept with technology as younger generations. Finally, nowadays there are too many apps one must use to track their health and communicate so making an all-in-one app will both address the concerns of the elderly and make it easier to use.


We hope that Elderly Helperly will be the one-stop solution where elderly people can both improve their health, feel connected with their loved ones and interact more with their community.

Research

To develop Elderly Helperly extensive research was conducted to find the best trends and features to implement in our application. Different publications were analyzed to find the best practices to develop apps for the elderly. In terms of colours to be chosen for the design, it is important to use colours with contrast for an app that is easier to navigate and use [1]. Furthermore, buttons and colors should be kept big so they are more readable and easier to interact with [1]. These are two key principles that we will be keeping in mind while designing our application. Additionally, it is important to make it clear what is clickable in the application so navigation is simpler for the elderly [2]. We will be implementing this by only making buttons clickable within our application and not using any clickable text.

To provide an alternative to loading data individually we have decided to implement an optional QR code reader that users can use to load bulk data. Our research showed that QR codes can help senior citizens by keeping more accurate records in terms of health [3]. Finally, we want to make sure that our users can easily navigate within our different screens therefore, we will be adding a back button to most of our screens. It is shown that this enhances user experience overall and can lead to less frustration when using the app [4]. Furthermore, the features we have chosen for our app are only what is required for users to achieve their goals and will make our application less complicated and more efficient. Finally, we have decided not to use icons in our application and decided to only use text to convey information. We believe this is the most efficient way of communicating with the elderly and research shows that is an area of concern for the elderly [5].

Survey

After extensive research was conducted we decided to develop a survey to gain insights on potential decisions we were going to make regarding the development of the application. A Google Survey was created that contained a mix of multiple choice, short answer and long answer that aimed at collecting data for us to use in our decision-making process. The survey and its disclosures can be seen here and below is our analysis of the information collected. Finally, all information that was collected remains confidential and the participants could remain anonymous.

The results of our survey have been divided into design and functionality-related questions for easier analysis.

Design

Below are the results for all design-related questions of the survey. The information is displayed in pie charts to visualize the percentage weight of each answer.


Click to zoom in and out.

From the data gathered for this part of the survey, it can be seen that the vast majority of people believe colour consistency is important when developing an application for the elderly. Furthermore, the best colour pallet to use is rainbow, this concurs with the research we conducted because it makes it easier to differentiate buttons. The participants believe that colour consistency is important when designing and that minimalism confuses the elderly. People also believe that button placement is important when developing an app for the elderly because it leads to less confusion. Additionally, they believe that clickable text confuses the elderly and this aligns with what we found in the research we conducted. Finally, people believe that icons confuse the elderly, this is most likely because of the lack of labels when using them.

Functionality

Below is the data for functionality-related questions of the survey along with the short and long answer questions.


Click to zoom in and out

In regards to how many functions our app should have, people believe that 1 to 5 is best and this is because it makes the app simpler to navigate. Furthermore, people believe that implementing videos is a good feature to have for the elderly. A more controversial functionality is QR codes as a slight minority believe it's a good feature to have for the elderly. Some of the most prominent answers in terms of best app features regarding elderly people's health were exercise and medication tracking. However, some of the most prominent answers for worst app features regarding elderly people's health were journaling and maps because of the risk of confusion. Finally, the most common struggles for elderly people regarding applications were small buttons and texts and complicated apps to navigate. All of this data collected will be used in the app development process of application to ensure we have the best product possible.

Personas

To better understand the userbase of our app, we created some personas of the expected demographic. We made sure to include a range of goals based off of our reseach. Furthermore, we included certain disabilities that can be expected from senior citizens.

Frankie

The first persona we made, Frankie, is a hard worker who likes to stay fit. He relies on others to help keep him on track, be it with his medications or his appointments. His years working with heavy equipment has rendered him hard of hearing.

Ginette

Ginette is a retiree with little remaining mobility. She's lived her whole life in french and would have a hard time using an english application. She would like an app that not only helps her keep up her exercises to improve her diminishing mobility, but would also require an app that caters to her degenerating sight. All the while, she aims to make some friends adn keep in touch with her family.

Guillaume

Guillaume is the only young persona of the bunch. While we were thinking about the demographic of the app, a thought came up: "Who else will use the app beside the elderly?". For this reason, we included a younger person who might help their grandparent or an elder they know set up their account on the app.

Theresa

Finally there's Theresa, who represents an elder in the younger range, who wants an app to help keep her fit and that keeps track of various merdications. She's pretty tech savvy and aims to reduce the cluter on her phone by combining finding an app that combines multiple functionalities.

Design

In the design portion, we added the colours from a colour palette we selected and prettied up the base wireframe. The buttons were rounded and drop shadows were added to the user interface to improve on the flat feeling of the design, giving it more depth. The colours were kept consistent by making all the inputs one colour and keeping the back and settings buttons the same throughout the application. Furthermore, we added a logo that we desgined to the login page.

Color scheme

The survey suggested both nude and rainbow colours would be best for our application; we went with two shades of beige for the background. The buttons and other interactive inputs of the app make use of a more varied rainbow aesthetic. We tried to keep the colours consistent throughout the app so that it wouldn't be too confusing.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the survey and research conducted have provided valuable insights into the expectations for our application and design. This mini-project not only enhanced our understanding of the needs of elderly individuals but also enlightened us on the most suitable user interface for them. As the population ages, the evolving technological landscape ensures that newer generations, more accustomed to the internet and associated technologies, will continue to shape user preferences. This experience has been instrumental in aligning our project with the evolving needs of a tech-savvy aging population.
